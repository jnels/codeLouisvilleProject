Ashes, Ashes

It is not the pull of the moon or the spinning
of the earth that drives the tide of sand

to the sea—desert begets desert, a city falls
into itself. A man cuts a tree to plant a field

to build a fire, and his house turns to sand
He butchers a cow, but already the vulture

has picked it clean. Of what use is a seed
where a weed will not grow, or a bucket

in a river of salt? Desert begets desert,
bone begets bone, a city falls into itself.

The Drinking Hole

The baboons are grunting
and barking like dogs,
distorting their faces
and shaking the trees.

They circle around us
clutching their fists
around sticks
and throwing stones.

Why are you swimming
down there in the river?
Why are you swimming
in our drinking hole?

We stand on a rock
surrounded by the current.
Not one of us dares
to jump into the stream.

The Body Washer

She wears the clothes of a dead woman—
a black robe and a faded head scarf.

"They're almost too fancy," she says
without glancing from the bullet wound.

"I try to stay simple. I don't care too much
about things." Her days are spent alone

with malformed babies, headless
professors, and faceless wives of diplomats.

"Everything has its time," she says
as she cuts a girl's clothing away, carefully

keeping the awrah hidden under a sheet.
She dips her hand into a bucket and begins

to wash—first the head, then the body.
"When you understand the unknown,

you can no longer call it your enemy—
the same bath, the same bowl."
